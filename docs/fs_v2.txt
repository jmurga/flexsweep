\abstract{Flex-sweep is a convolutional neural network-based method able to detect a wide range of selective sweeps signals, including those thousands of generations old, from single-species genomic data, while being robust to background selection. Here we present a more general-purpose tool that streamlines the workflow, vastly speeds up summary-statistic computation, relax CNN constraints by supporting custom architectures and haplotype sorting methods, enable polarization while being highly customizable and robust to model mis-specification making it particularly well-suited to non-model organism analysis. Flex-sweep now scales to thousands of simulated training scenarios and enables genome-wide inference on a standard workstation rather than an HPC.}

\section{Introduction}
Quantifying the contribution of selective sweeps to genomic adaptation remains an active area of research in population genetics, and methods able to detect diverse sweeps remain a challenge \citep{kern_diploshic_2018,xue_discovery_2021,whitehouse_timesweeper_2023,whitehouse_tree_2024,lauterbur_versatile_2023,vaughn_fast_2024}. In recent years, Machine Learning (ML) techniques have provided robust and adaptable alternatives to most typical approaches, such as classical population genetic summary statistics \citep{kim_linkage_2004,voight_map_2006,garud_recent_2015,akbari_identifying_2018}, maximum likelihood \citep{williamson_localizing_2007,degiorgio_sweepfinder2_2016,harris_likelihood_2020}, composite multiple signals \citep{grossman_composite_2010,sugden_localization_2018,alachiotis_raisd_2018} or Approximate Bayesian Computation (ABC) \citep{peter_distinguishing_2012,racimo_test_2014,johri_developing_2023} approaches. Many different ML-based approaches can compare, discern, and learn intrinsic patterns over abstract haplotype images or preprocessed summary statistics, including SVM, RNN, CNN, GNN, DANN among others \citep{pavlidis_searching_2010,kern_diploshic_2018,hejase_deep-learning_2022,mo_domain-adaptive_2023,van_den_belt_fast_2025,whitehouse_tree_2024}. Such methods are usually exploited as an image classifier fed with labelled simulated neutral and non-neutral regions, recognising local patterns that can be used to classify empirical genomic regions into different classes \citep{kern_diploshic_2018,caldas_inference_2022,whitehouse_tree_2024}. Generally, supervised learning methods can learn local patterns across genomic positions and/or statistics, depending on whether they are fed using raw or rearranged haplotype matrices \citep{arnab_efficient_2025, van_den_belt_fast_2025, zhao_data_2025}, as well as feature vectors of classical population genetics summary statistics \citep{kern_diploshic_2018, caldas_inference_2022, lauterbur_versatile_2023}. Moreover, due to the advent of efficient software to infer genealogies at the genome level \citep{kelleher_inferring_2019,speidel_method_2019,deng_robust_2024}, newer ML methods based on the information from the ancestral recombination graph are being developed \citep{hejase_deep-learning_2022,mo_domain-adaptive_2023,whitehouse_tree_2024}).

Although many existing methods have been shown to be able to detect selective sweeps, most of them are limited to specific types of sweeps regarding time, frequency and strength. Moreover, detecting older sweeps with subtle signals is particularly challenging, despite detecting the earliest dates of selective events has recently become an important goal of population geneticists, especially for humans \citep{lauterbur_versatile_2023}. Nonetheless, methods for such a specific task typically rely on correlated allele frequency differences between populations, limiting selective sweep scans to populations with genomic data for outgroups and/or admixture populations \citep{racimo_test_2014,key_human_2016,racimo_testing_2016, cheng_fast_2017,peyregne_detecting_2017}. For non-model organisms where the quality or availability of multiple populations' genomic and/or outgroup data are limited, the detection of older sweeps became highly unfeasible.

Flex-sweep is a CNN-based model that is a Swiss-knife army to detect sweeps of different types, strengths and especially age in comparison to other competitors \citep{kern_diploshic_2018,arnab_efficient_2025}. It was primarily designed to be flexible in terms of the types, strengths, and ages of sweeps, requiring a single phased population while being robust to background selection, expanding the ability to easily discern past selective pressures such as ancient epidemics in model and non-model species \citep{lauterbur_versatile_2023}. Here, we present a newer, faster and more flexible Flex-sweep update that simplifies and streamlines the project structure, files, simulations, summary statistics estimation and training. We aim to extend Flex-sweep to general purpose tool to genome-wide selection scans, not only improving its original CNN
implementation but extending to features that facilitate genome-wide positive selection on non-model species, including multiple new summary statistics implementation, common plotting utilities, haplotype matrices support, robustness to mis-specification, fast and easy implementation of genome-wide outlier approaches, polarization and sweep enrichment ranking algorithm.

\section{Pipeline and new features}\label{sec2}
Flex-Sweep first version required to create a complex project structure, in addition to changing prior parameters on non-standardized custom input files, and manually installing external software dependencies. To improve the efficiency, robustness and reproducibility when using Flex-sweep, we refactored it as a user-friendly, well-documented Python package with a Command Line Interface (CLI).

To show the improvements, we tested Flex-sweep regarding multiple training simulations sets, and predicting 1000GP human populationa \citep{byrska-bishop_high-coverage_2022}, moving from HPC to a common workstation requirements. We also tested timing and scalability to a widely use summary statistic CNN-based (see Computational Resources and Requirements section). Similarly to the first version, Flex-sweep works in three main steps: simulation, summary statistics estimation (feature vectors), and training/classification.

\subsection{Simulations}
The simulations module now takes advantage of \texttt{demes} \citep{gower_demes_2022} to simulate custom demography histories, and the software includes a \texttt{discoal} pre-compiled binary \citep{kern_discoal_2016} (last accessed 4 September 2025), to avoid the installation of external dependencies (custom installation can be easily run by just input the binary path). Priors parameters are flexible to different distribution configurations and do not require manually fitting a custom file, but any option is documented and can be easily accessed through the Python API or CLI.

\subsection{Feature vector estimation}
We refactor the entire feature vector module, focusing on speed and traceability to avoid large amounts of intermediate files, which previously required high data storage for simple runs, as well as external software installation to estimate specific summary statistics. Therefore, the estimation of the entire feature vector set relies on Numpy and \texttt{scikit-allel} data structures, a well-documented and community-used Python packages \citep{alistair_miles_cgghscikit-allel_2024}. The summary statistics estimation was refactored taking advantage of Numba and Numpy vectorization for all previous statistics except for: nS\textsubscript{L} and iHS, which rely on \texttt{scikit-allel} functions. Therefore, we not only included optimized versions of the newer statistics presented in Flex-sweep (Sratio, hapDAF-o/s, highfreq and lowfreq) \citep{lauterbur_versatile_2023}, but also iSAFE \citep{akbari_identifying_2018}, DIND \citep{barreiro_evolutionary_2009}, and the previously modified H12 \citep{garud_recent_2015} and HAF \citep{ronen_predicting_2015} statistics.

All the summary statistic outputs and feature vectors now rely on \texttt{Polars} DataFrames, which substantially reduces the performance cost of feature vector estimation and normalisation associated with I/O computations. The new approach avoids the creation of a vast number of intermediate files or inaccessible summary statistics values, a complex project structure, and high data storage requirements, allowing us to easily inspect summary statistic outputs while reducing RAM consumption. This way, the workflow remains fully traceable, while raw classical summary statistics can be easily output. The new configuration allow us to extend Flex-sweep not only work with CNN but we also included a module to perform classical outlier approach through empirical distributions \citep{akey_constructing_2009}, as well as easily retrieve raw information at SNPs or window level in case the user needs any complementary analysis, providing and extra layer of explanatory and visualization analyses.

Because we intend to provide a flexible approach testing for selective sweeps, users can use now any custom combination of the summary statistics included within the package. In addition to the above mentioned, we refactor and include several others summary statistics (see Table 1; note that any other statistic already available in \texttt{scikit-allel} can be used straightforwardly). Importantly, the software supports user-defined genomic intervals and window sizes, enabling feature extraction across highly customizable regions. This design allows users to fit the analysis for the most informative combination of genomic regions and summary statistics describing the organism to analyze. Although primarly designed to detect positive selection, we also included two widely used statistics designed to detect balancing selection using only polymorphic data (see Table 1). In combination with other statistics, Flex-sweep could also provide an easy interface to detect recent balancing selection (see \cite{isildak_distinguishing_2021} for further discussion on balancing selection analysis using Neural Networks).


\subsection{Normalization}
Recombination rate heterogeneity has been shown to bias population genetic inferences broadly, including genome-wide outlier scans, ancestral recombination graph inference, and effective population size estimation \citep{booker_variation_2020, johnson_patterns_2018, ishigohoka_high-recombining_2025}. In the context of sweep detection, haplotype-based statistics also exhibit spurious correlations with local recombination rate that can only be addressed through joint stratification by allele frequency and recombination rate \citep{johnson_patterns_2018}. Following the \cite{johnson_patterns_2018} approach, Flex-sweep now incorporates recombination-rate-stratified normalization of input statistics whenever a high-quality recombination map is available. Specifically, each input statistic is standardized within bins defined by the joint combination of window position, window center, and local recombination rate, extending the original normalization scheme to explicitly account for recombination rate heterogeneity across the genome:

\begin{equation}
    Z\text{-}score = \pi_i^{(w_1,c_1,r)}=\frac{\pi_i^{(w_1,c_1,r)} - \mu^{(w_1,c_1,r)}}{\sigma^{(w_1,c_1,r)}}
\end{equation}

where $\pi_i$ is a given statistic, and $\mu^{(w_1,c_1,r)}$ and $\sigma^{(w_1,c_1,r)}$ are the mean and standard deviation computed across all windows sharing the same window size $w_1$, center $c_1$, and recombination rate bin $ r \in {1,…,R}$, with bin boundaries defined from the empirical recombination rate distribution.

For SNP-based statistics, normalization is performed analogously but conditional on derived allele frequency (DAF) and local recombination rate. Specifically, each SNP-level statistic is first standardized within bins defined by the joint distribution of DAF and recombination rate:

\begin{equation}
\pi_{i(std)} = \pi_i^{(f,r)}=\frac{\pi_i^{(f,r)} - \mu^{(f,r)}}{\sigma^{(f,r)}}
\end{equation}

where $f$ denotes the derived allele frequency bin of SNP $i$, and $\mu^{(f,r)}$ and $\sigma^{(f,r)}$ are the mean and standard deviation computed across all SNPs falling within the same DAF bin $f$ and recombination rate bin $r$.

\subsection{Training and prediction}
Because feature vectors are now flexible to any combination of statistics, genomic centers, and window sizes, the new API automatically adapts to custom CNN architectures, supporting both 1D and 2D convolutions. To further enable compatibility with haplotype image-based approaches, we incorporated a comprehensive set of haplotype-matrix sorting and rearrangement strategies prior to model training. Sorting the columns (SNP positions) of the input matrix by derived allele frequency, correlation coefficient, occurrence frequency, or subregional bipartite correlation imposes a consistent structure that has been shown to improve CNN classification accuracy across diverse confounding scenarios, including population bottlenecks, migration, and recombination hotspots \citep{zhao_data_2025}. In addition, several disruption-based orderings — targeting genomic position, site frequency spectrum (SFS), linkage disequilibrium (LD), and allele frequency perturbations, including median-based orderings — enable systematic assessment of which population genetic features drive model predictions \citep{tran_interpreting_2025,zhao_data_2025}. Together, these additions standardize haplotype preprocessing and improve compatibility with other commonly used ML frameworks.

\subsection{DANN}
Flex-sweep is now more versatile to analyse non-model organisms where the quality or availability of simulated parameters, such as the mutation rate, recombination rate, and demography, is limited. We extended the method to work with Domain Adaptive model proposed by \cite{mo_domain-adaptive_2023}. Domain Adaptive Neural Network trains not only using labeled simulated data as expected for a CNN, but also incorporates empirical unlabeled data during the training. The goal then is to generalize the classification task across any feature distorting simulated feature vector distributions from real data by learning a shared representation pattern that is highly predictive for the CNN classifier but uninformative about the domain. In the case of ML approaches like CNN, trained models under unrealistic demography, recombination, or mutation landscape can easily confound sweep prediction due to the overfitting of artifacts. The Domain Adaptive model implemented is explicitly designed to account for and mitigate such a mismatch between simulated and real data. Note that when working with extremely out-of-range demographics (e.g, training over constant population sizes) or simulated parameters, DA implementation may still perform worse than the original CNN \citep{mo_domain-adaptive_2023}.

To improve robustness under domain shift, we further stabilize adversarial domain adaption through controlled gradient scheduling and constrained adversarial strength. Rather than applying a fixed gradient reversal factor throughout the training, we introduce a time-epoch dependent schedule in which the adversarial signal is gradually increased from zero to a predefined maximum. This allows the model to first learn task-discriminative features from labeled simulated data before progressively enforcing domain invariance using unlabeled empirical data. In addition, constraining the maximum adversarial strength prevents the domain objective from dominating the optimization process, preserving features that remain informative for classification despite being partially domain-specific. Together, this strategy improves the stability of optimization, reduces negative transfer, and promotes the learning of representations that generalize more effectively across mismatched empirical and simulated genomic distributions.

\subsection{Polarization}
Based on the findings of \cite{hernandez_context_2007} on the impact of ancestral mis-specification in tests for positive selection, Flex-sweep refactors and extends \texttt{est-sfs} \citep{keightley_inferring_2018} to support straightforward allele polarization. The user can automatically retrieve multiple outgroup sequences from Multi-Alignment Format (MAF) files to infer ancestral-state. Given a focal-species polymorphism dataset (VCF) and MAF file, we adapted \texttt{maftk} \citep{buffalo_vsbuffalomaftk_2025} parsing layer to work with a streaming merge-join algorithm, so the refactored pipeline i) subsets up to three outgroup species from the alignment ii) extracts the aligned outgroup bases at polymorphic sites, and iii) computes posterior probabilities per-site ancestral-state following \cite{keightley_inferring_2018}. These posteriors are then used to probabilistically assign ancestral and derived states for both major and minor alleles, enabling downstream selection scans while removing potential false positive signals due to ancestral mis-specification. We aim to exploit the advent of large comparative genomic resources—such as the Zoonomia Project \citep{armstrong_progressive_2020,kuderna_identification_2024}—together with the growing ability to incorporate additional species into whole-genome alignments \citep{armstrong_progressive_2020} to facilitate curated selection scans in non-model species.

\subsection{Rank and enrichment}
We included a rank algorithm for post-processing sweep probabilities and associated genomic elements. The function relies on the Python package \texttt{polars-bio} \citep{wiewiorka_polars-biofast_2025} to quickly estimate genomic distances between the predicted genomic region and the element listed in a BED file to associate the nearest sweep prediction. To assess whether highly ranked elements are enriched within predefined genomic categories, we implemented the enrichment pipeline described in \cite{enard_ancient_2020}, which tests for significant enrichment among top-ranked sweeps genome-wide.

Enrichment is evaluated across multiple rank thresholds by comparing the number of case elements at or below each threshold to matched control sets. Control sets are generated via an iterative bootstrap procedure that samples controls based on the mean of multiple confounding factors relative to the case set, while controlling for distance and tolerance range. Confounding factors can be flexibly defined by the user, and variables with non-monotonic relationships to sweep prevalence can be excluded from matching. At each threshold, enrichment is summarized as the ratio of case to mean control counts, with confidence intervals derived from the distribution across control sets.

To assign false discovery rate (FDR) to the full enrichment curve, rather than to individual thresholds, we generate a null distribution by block-shuffling sweep signals across the genome. Genomic elements, ordered by chromosomal position, are partitioned into contiguous segments, with segment boundaries optionally adjusted to preserve local clustering of high-ranking sweep signals. Segments are then randomly reordered and independently mirrored, preserving spatial clustering while disrupting associations with case labels. The full counting procedure is applied to each shuffled replicate using the same bootstrap control sets as in the observed data, ensuring consistent bias between real and null estimates. The FDR p-value is defined as the proportion of null replicates whose test statistic—computed as the sum of case-minus-control mean differences across all thresholds—equals or exceeds the observed value.

\section{Application to human data}
We tested Flex-sweep on the entire 1000GP dataset. For an easier comparison with the previous version, we present here the results on the Yoruba (YRI) population. Because Flex-sweep is now able to handle larger training set, we simulated 250,000 training neutral and non-neutral regions of 1.2MB, respectively, instead of the original 22,000 sets. We input the mutation rate ($\mu$) uniformly at random within the interval $[5\times10^{-9},\, 2\times10^{-8}]$ \citep{smith_large_2018}, the per-site per-generation recombination rate $r$ from from an exponential distribution with mean of $1\times10^{-8}$ truncated at range $[1\times10^{-9},\, 1\times10^{-7}]$. Sweep simulations accounted for uniformly distributed sweep ages of $[0,5000]$ generations, selection strength of $[0.01,0.05]$, start allele frequency $[0,0.1]$ and end allele frequency of $[0.5,1]$, accounting for multiple sweep signals.

Figure \ref{fig:yri_manhattan} shows Manhattan plot for YRI population genome-wide sweep predictions. Similarly to \cite{lauterbur_versatile_2023}, we tested whether the newer implementation identifies true positive candidates on the YRI genome by comparing the occurrence of sweeps at virus-interacting proteins (VIPs) where a strong excess of sweeps has previously been reported \citep{schrider_soft_2017,enard_ancient_2020}. We employed the rank algorithm included within the software to consider genes subject to a sweep, to compare the number of HIV VIPs in sweeps with the average number in random control sets to obtain the fold enrichment and FDR. Similarly to \cite{lauterbur_versatile_2023}, the enrichment of VIPs in high-confidence sweep classifications (see Figure \ref{fig:hiv_enrichment}).

The newer version allows us to easily simulate and predict the entire 1000GP dataset \citep{byrska-bishop_high-coverage_2022} (see Supplementary material). Note that we take advantage of the Flex-sweep improvements to easily parallelise and fast predict genome-wide signals over the 26 populations by processing on our own server (see next section for further discussion).

\section{Computational Resources and Requirements}
We developed the newer Flex-sweep on a workstation equipped with an AMD Ryzen 9 PRO 5945 12-core CPU with 64 GB of RAM. The amount of time and memory usage taken for feature vectors estimation would lastly rely on the species genome, sample size and chosen statistics. Note that we show here YRI population results since it is one of the populations with a higher sample size (n=108) and nucleotide diversity levels.

Because Flex-sweep is now designed to estimate custom summary statistics and genomic ranges when processing feature vectors, we compare performance to \texttt{diploS/HIC} \citep{kern_diploshic_2018}, one of the most widely used CNN-based methods for detecting selection (last accesion 12/03/26). Note that Flex-sweep and \texttt{diploS/HIC} differ in the normalization step, however, such an step is supposed to be negligible in time in comparison with summary statistics estimation and I/O operations regarding the number of simulations or VCF's windows processed.

For YRI-like simulations, we estimated 180 summary statistics by replica. For a fair comparison, we modified \texttt{diploS/HIC haploid} mode to parallelize computations similarly to Flex-sweep batching since \texttt{diploS/HIC} CLI works sequentially at both simulation files and genome-wide windowing. We set up \texttt{diploS/HIC haploid} mode to account for same Flex-sweep assumptions on phased and missing data. Note, we estimated 12 subwindows instead of the default 11 subwindows because of our 1.2 MB simulations. Figure \ref{fig:runtime} shows summary statistic  mean time execution and the relative improvement between \texttt{diploS/HIC} and the new Flex-sweep integration for 22,000 neutral and non-neutrla simulations and multiple threads configurations, achieving up to two to five-fold improvement. Similary we compared runtime on genome-wide YRI computation using 1000GP high coverage data too. We followed diploS/HIC default configuration so the VCF were slide into 1e5

To speed up the analysis on the 26 populations on 1000GP dataset, presented above, we parallelise the feature vectors estimations on a server accounting for 2 AMD EPYC 7713 64-Core CPUs with 4 TB of RAM. Therefore we also compare thread scalibilty by comparing Flex-sweep and diploshic.

The training and prediction were performed on a RTX 3080 with 10 GB of VRAM. The training process for YRI took $\approx30$ minutes using 250,000 neutral and non-neutral feature vectors respectively. The training incorporates early stopping to prevent overfitting and converges in about 40 epochs.

For YRI simulations, we estimated 180 summary statistics by replica using the default diploS/HIC haploid mode, accounting for phased data and taking on average ≈ 3 seconds. Note, we estimated 12 subwindows instead of the default 11 subwindows because of our 1.2 MB simulations. When customising Flex-sweep to estimate the same set of summary statistics, it only took ≈ 0.15 seconds due to Numba and Numpy vectorization when refactoring statistics. Note that both scenarios, default feature vector or diploS/HIC-like processing, took less than the state-of-the-art TrIdent CNN processing raw haplotype matrices, which takes 6.83 seconds to generate an image [Arnab et al., 2024]
